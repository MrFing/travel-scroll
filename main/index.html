MY TRAVEL SCROLL — A Journey Through Time

Travelling across India from 1947 to 2026 — culture, people, cities & life transformed

Imagine stepping off a bullock cart in 1947 India — a land of dusty, unhurried towns where women wore handwoven saris, men worked the land with wooden ploughs, and the nearest school was miles away down an unpaved road. Cities like Bombay and Calcutta were congested colonial ports; Delhi was still finding its identity as the seat of a newly born republic. The air smelled of jute, marigold, and wood smoke. Most people did not own a radio, let alone a telephone. Life expectancy hovered at a fragile 32 years, and roughly 88 in every 100 adults could not read the words on a ballot paper.

Now leap forward to 2026. The bullock cart has been replaced by a metro rail gliding silently through glass-and-steel skylines. The same women — or their granddaughters — scroll social media on smartphones while managing businesses, voting in elections, and exploring careers unthinkable a generation ago. Indian cinema has gone from black-and-white regional films to a global phenomenon; street food once eaten out of clay pots is now Instagrammed from designer cafés. Festivals still blaze with colour, but they now stream live to millions worldwide. Where once 14 provinces governed a fragile dominion, 28 states and 8 union territories form a constitutional republic — the world's largest democracy — with a space programme that landed on the Moon's south pole in 2023.

The journey from then to now is not only a story of numbers and policies. It is a story of cloth becoming code — handloom giving way to software; of silence becoming signal — 120,000 telephone lines expanding to 1.16 billion mobile subscribers; of hunger becoming aspiration — a nation once dependent on food imports becoming a leading agricultural exporter. India's cultural soul — its temples, its classical dance, its devotion, its argument — has remained the thread, even as everything woven around it changed beyond recognition.
